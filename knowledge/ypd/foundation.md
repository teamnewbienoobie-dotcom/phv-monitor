INTRODUCING STRENGTH TRAINING TO YOUNG ATHLETES

At this early stage the main aim is to teach the basic movement patterns of squat, lunge, push, pull, hinge, brace and rotate to a level where the athlete can perform them through a full range of movement and understand the technical requirements of each of those movements under bodyweight. 

In addition, where possible, we should be challenging the athletes to think about where we see these movements occur in their sports in order to ‘connect the dots’ between what we are doing from a strength and conditioning perspective and their own sports. This understanding is critical to the athlete engaging in the training process. 

Exercises will be versus bodyweight only at the beginning, with a focus on hitting key technical points (e.g. hips below knees in a squat, back straight etc). Athletes will work in pairs/threes and perform the role of the athlete and coach using these technical points to assess performance and should be encouraged to provide constructive feedback to their partners.

Power is another one of the key areas for development identified in the Youth Physical Development model for young athletes (Lloyd & Oliver, 2012).

Power is the ability to develop force rapidly (force x velocity).

In most sports, the time is not available to generate maximum force (i.e. strength) the movements and skills must be executed in a very short period of time meaning that the required force must be generated very quickly in order to be successful.

Across our LTAD curriculum we emphasize strength development, but it has greater importance earlier in the system, as athletes the amount of power an athlete can produce will be limited if maximum force (strength) capacity is low.

In novices and relatively weak athletes that are new to resistance training (as in the Foundation/Development Phases) strength training has been shown to improve both strength and power. Whereas power training has been shown to increase power only. In the study, both training groups (heavy strength only vs ballistic exercises only) improve performance in the jumping test but the adaptations and mechanisms by which they improved were specific to the imposed demands (Cormie et al. 2010)

What this means is that we don’t want to all our eggs in one basket and just develop strength. We need to spend time developing both, with the emphasis shifting at different times of the pathway and depending on the athlete’s needs.

It is extremely important that as coaches we provide opportunities for athletes to learn to produce force rapidly in a variety of different movements and in all planes of movement (sagittal, front and transverse) in jumping, leaping, hopping, throwing; and later on using loaded ballistic & weightlifting movements.

INTRODUCTION

One of the most important lessons to learn in LTAD is that it is absolutely critical to get your athletes out of the weight room and give them the freedom and opportunity to learn how to apply the strength and power they’ve been building in their to sports generic and sports specific movements that they use in their actual sports.

For 99% of young athletes, their sports are NOT weight lifting competitions. We know lifting weights helps their performance, but it is not the be all and end all. We must get them out into a sports hall, gymnasium, field, track, astro or whatever open space you have available and start building movement skills that carry over into what, at the age of 11-15, is probably multiple sports.

Agility & Speed Training is one of our most powerful tools for engaging the athletes in the LTAD process long term, particularly when you employ the concept of TGfU (Teaching Games for Understanding) to this particular modality.

In our experience this is as powerful as using modalities like Parkour for introducing jumping and landing, when it comes to engagement and it is crucial to break what can be a relatively monotonous process for a younger athlete if we are solely focused on getting stronger in the gym.

We will still address strength and power during times where agility is more in focus, but the methods maybe different and it’s likely happening in a lower frequency or volume during this period.

## AGILITY MOVEMENT TECHNICAL MODELS

**內容摘要**
AGILITY MOVEMENT TECHNICAL MODELS
READY POSITION

Technical Points:

1. Feet shoulder width, weight should be on the ball of the foot.

2. A ‘credit card’ should be able to fit under the heel.

3. Lower the centre of mass, bending at the ankles, knees and hips.

4. Shoulders over the knees maintain a neutral spine position

6. Athlete should be looking forward with their hands

7. Feet can be static or dynamic

DECELERATION

Technical Points:

1. Shorten steps

2. Lower the centre of mass and shift weight / lean back slightly

3. Brake using a flat foot, contacts should be out in front of your body.

4. Finish in the ready position* to be able to re-accelerate in any direction

*Alternatively, athletes can use the following:

a) Lunge stop - typically used when the athlete thinks they are going to either need to backpedal or accelerate forwards again quickly.

b) Side on Ready Position (with left or right foot forward) - typically used when the athlete is pressing a defender and they are trying to shepherd them in a certain direction or they anticipate that is the direction they are going to need to track them.

DIRECTIONAL STEP

Technical Points:

1. Trail leg is powerfully driven into the ground in the opposite direction to that which the athlete intends to travel.

2. Athlete’s hips should turn into the direction of movement.

3. Lead foot is ‘unloaded’ & turns into the direction of movement

4. Bodyweight shifts over the lead leg.

5. Body continues to shift forward and rotate into an acceleration position.6. Athlete proceeds to transition into linear acceleration mechanics.

HIP TURN

Technical Points:

1. Athlete turns the hips in the direction of movement and drives the rear leg (in relation to direction of movement) powerfully into the ground in the opposite direction to that of intended travel.

2. Create an effective pushing angle with the knee inside ankle, hip inside knee, shoulder inside hip.

3. COM shifts over the lead leg, and continues moving forward to create an effective acceleration angle.

4. Drop step (i.e. the leg lead leg) must not place foot ahead of COM.

5. Effective arm drive that helps generate ground forces.

LATERAL SHUFFLE

Technical Points:

1. Steps initiated from the outside leg.

2. Feet remain low to the ground, and maintain a position wider than the knee, which is wider than the hips.

3. Feet must stay apart

4. Centre of mass stays low, and the athlete should not rise and fall

5. Bodyweight on the ball of the feet.

WELCOME TO THE WEIGHT ROOM
For some kids, when you take them to the weight room, it’s like the first time their parents took them to Toys R’ US. INTRODUCTION

The ability to express high power outputs is considered to be a foundational quality for successful performance in a range of sporting movements including jumping, sprinting, throwing and changing direction (Haff and Nimphius, 2012). Many movements in sport require the ability to produce high amounts of force in a short period of time and a high rate of force development is often related to an athlete’s strength levels (Stone, et. al, 2002). 

It is clear there are a range of benefits from developing strength with resistance training as it impacts on many other qualities including power, speed and agility. However, in the research by Cormie et al. (2010) ballistic power training had desirable effects that were specific to that training stimulus, not brought about by the heavy strength training. So, whilst our relatively weak, youth or novice athletes need to develop strength to enable them to achieve higher power outputs. It is probably most beneficial to take a mixed method approach to impact both the force and velocity components of the power equation. Providing athletes with power training exercises allows them to translate gains in strength into the ability to express force (Oliver and Rumpf, 2012).

In strength training using heavier resistances (relative to the athletes level) involves the muscles working slowly to overcome the load. Whilst increasing strength enhances force production and power up to a point, it doesn’t adequately enhance specific components of explosive movements such as sprinting that require rapid activation of muscles and rapid switching from eccentric to concentric work (Siff, 2003).

MEDICINE BALL THROWS
Ballistic training uses jumping and throwing movements where there is a continuous acceleration throughout the concentric phase. For example: medicine ball throws, Olympic Lifting and Loaded Jumping exercises. A wide spectrum of loads low, moderate and high can be used to target different aspects of the force-velocity curve. With heavier loads (i.e. cleans & jerks, high load used to target the strength-speed range, and lighter loads for the speed-strength range.

MEDICINE BALL THROWS

Medicine Ball throws provide an excellent training tool as they are appropriate for athletes of all ages, stages of maturity, levels of ability and sports. As a general tool, they are excellent for providing athletes with an opportunity to express their strength being developed in the weight room in an explosive manner. These exercises are also great for helping athletes to learn to coordinate the transfer of force from the lower body, through the trunk to the upper body. They can also be used in a more specific manner too, by selecting movements that are specific to various actions within different sports.

The throws can be performed in a variety of ways. Some are better thrown into open space, others with a partner or using a wall to rebound the ball off. From experience, with partner medicine ball drills the quality of the feed from the partner is key. When using partner fed drills with young athletes the drills are generally messy because the feed is inconsistent and poorly executed. It is better to have the athlete ‘feed’ themselves from a wall than another athlete. When working with large groups it is important to make sure you set them out in sufficient space to avoid people getting hit by a heavy med ball. Setting the athletes out on a line and getting them to throw then collect as a group is advisable. They can also throw to a partner opposite them but ensure the partner is positioned further away than the athlete is able to throw so the ball will not hit them.

## LOADED JUMPS

LOADED JUMPS

Loaded jumping in the form of jump squats (i.e. with a countermovement jump) and squat jumps (i.e. with a 2-4 second pause between the eccentric and concentric contractions) with external load are an excellent tool for improving jumping ability and increasing power outputs (Sheppard, 2014). Loaded jumps are can allow us to get an effective a power training stimulus without the higher technical demands of Olympic Lifting, and are useful for a number of reasons:

1. They are easy to execute and great to use when tracking velocity and/or power output with VBT.

2. If you have an athlete join your pathway late (e.g. 15-16 years old) you can train power using these exercises whilst they learn Olympic Lifts.

3. A great alternative for athletes that have an upper body injury or movement restriction that prevents them getting the bar into a front squat rack position or overhead squat position required for Olympic Lifts.

**內容摘要**
THE IMPACT OF GROWH AND MATURATION

As with linear speed, rapid changes in height and weight will effect change of direction and agility. It means that there is a requirement to provide opportunities to the athlete to refine and recalibrate their technique in the agility/COD movement skills throughout the adolescent growth spurt.

The fact the athlete becomes faster at PHV naturally, plus 9-10kg heavier at PWV means that they are now coming into any directional changes with higher entry velocities and carrying greater momentum (velocity x mass = momentum). The table below highlights some typical changes in height (+~9cm/year), weight (+~9kg/year) and 10m speed (-0.15s) that occur during the growth spurt and how this translates into a 30% increase in momentum (225 kg·m/s to 291.6 kg·m/s).

In addition to increases in momentum, the changes in stature mean the athlete will also need to adjust their technique to create effective lines of force to brake, change direction and reaccelerate. The athlete will have a higher centre of mass, longer legs, and they will need to find slightly different positions to change direction effectively.

The best way to address this is to keep these movements in throughout the period of rapid growth and allow them to learn to gradually adapt the movements throughout the period they are growing rapidly. If you leave agility and COD direction (or any skills out) during this stage of their development, you are likely to end up with something that looks like a baby giraffe when they return to these movements.

Another factor to consider, if you have an athlete that has a growth related injury (e.g. osgood-schlatters) is that hard accelerations and decelerations may aggravate these types of injuries. Therefore, managing how much training of this nature they are exposed to may be necessary. If the athlete is in a period where they are competing and already loaded up with change of direction/agility tasks adding more in may exacerbate the issue.

DECELERATION

Technical Points:

1. Shorten steps

2. Lower the centre of mass and shift weight / lean back slightly

3. Brake using a flat foot, contacts should be out in front of your body.

4. Finish in the ready position* to be able to re-accelerate in any direction

*Alternatively, athletes can use the following:

a) Lunge stop - typically used when the athlete thinks they are going to either need to backpedal or accelerate forwards again quickly.

b) Side on Ready Position (with left or right foot forward) - typically used when the athlete is pressing a defender and they are trying to shepherd them in a certain direction or they anticipate that is the direction they are going to need to track them.

DIRECTIONAL STEP

Technical Points:

1. Trail leg is powerfully driven into the ground in the opposite direction to that which the athlete intends to travel.

2. Athlete’s hips should turn into the direction of movement.

3. Lead foot is ‘unloaded’ & turns into the direction of movement

4. Bodyweight shifts over the lead leg.

5. Body continues to shift forward and rotate into an acceleration position.6. Athlete proceeds to transition into linear acceleration mechanics.

HIP TURN

Technical Points:

1. Athlete turns the hips in the direction of movement and drives the rear leg (in relation to direction of movement) powerfully into the ground in the opposite direction to that of intended travel.

4. Drop step (i.e. the leg lead leg) must not place foot ahead of COM.

5. Effective arm drive that helps generate ground forces.

2. Feet remain low to the ground, and maintain a position wider than the knee, which is wider than the hips.

3. Feet must stay apart

4. Centre of mass stays low, and the athlete should not rise and fall

2.1 Philosophical Approaches to Training
Multi-Lateral Training Philosophy

Physical abilities outlined in our YPD model are the fundamental qualities essential to enhancing physical development and athletic performance. The physical abilities are listed and defined below:

1. Strength: The ability to generate force.

2. Power: The ability to express force rapidly

2. Speed: The ability to move an object or body rapidly.

3. Energy System Development (Endurance): The ability to sustain a workload.

4. Agility: The ability to change direction quickly in response to a stimulus.

5. Flexibility/mobility: The ability to move body parts through large ranges of motion.

The Multi-lateral Training Philosophy is an approach to training by which all of the physical abilities are addressed in a balanced manner. Every athletic endeavor involves some balance of the five biomotor abilities. Therefore, training should address each of these qualities to the degree necessary for the demands of the sport.

The YPD model developed by Lloyd & Oliver (2012) and our own model, both apply a multi-lateral training philosophy by training “all things, all the time” from childhood to adulthood. 

If we think about each physical ability having a “bucket” that needs to be filled. Each bucket is generally relatively low or empty when we commence a long-term development program. Through the Foundation and Development phase, we are aiming to fill all the buckets to provide a very good to excellent level across all qualities for their age, maturity status and training history. In the Performance phase, a more targeted approach is required to fill the buckets that are essential to meeting a specific sport’s demands at an elite level.

The basic premise of a philosophy of multisystem training is that over time, all these body systems should be addressed in some planned balance. 2.2 Stress & Adaptation

In the context of training theory for athletic development, stress is best understood as stimuli that impact allostasis or the currently maintained internal environment of the body. It is important to note that stress is multi-dimensional, having physical, mental, and emotional components (Butler, 1993). Thus, stress comes in many forms outside of training, such as job-related demands, relationship issues, sleep deprivation, and poor nutrition. All of these non-training stressors contribute to the athlete’s overall stress levels and impact the body’s response to training (Haff, 2016).

Adaptation

Adaptation is an adjustment made by the body in response to a change in the environment in order to better tolerate the new conditions (Zatsiorsky, 2006).

The SAID Principle

The SAID Principle stands for “Specific Adaptation to Imposed Demand.” This means that training adaptations are not randomly occurring but are dictated by the demands placed upon the body (Sheppard, 2016).

Principle of Reversibility

The Principle of Reversibility describes the loss of training adaptations following the removal of the training stimulus. This concept is also referred to as Detraining.

Time Frames for Adaptation

Generally, it takes multiple exposures to a training stimulus for adaptation to occur. Additionally, the various systems of the body adapt at different rates. For example, the skeletal system adapts slowly, while the cardiovascular system adapts rapidly. Adaptations that take longer to develop generally will take longer to deteriorate as well. In contrast, fast-adapting systems generally decline more rapidly. The length of time that a training adaptation is retained without training is known as a Training Residual. It is worth noting that endurance adaptations tend to decline more rapidly than strength adaptations.

For example, stroke volume has been found to decrease 10-17% in 12-21 days in trained athletes, and a 12-27% reduction in aerobic enzymes can occur in less than 4 weeks. In contrast, strength may be maintained up to 4 weeks after training cessation (Mujika & Padilla, 2000). It is important to consider these timeframes for key body systems and biomotor abilities to ensure that training cessation does not diminish performance to a significant degree during competitive periods.

2.3 Training Principles
Overload

Overload is the application of a training load greater in magnitude than what the athlete is accustomed to, typically in the form of increased volume and/or intensity (Zatsiorsky, 2006). Overload is a necessity in triggering the adaptive process, as loads below the athlete’s baseline are insufficient in stimulating adaptation. It is important to note that overload should not be achieved indiscriminately, but through a planned, progressive process.

For the athlete to adapt positively from training, the training load must fall between the Minimum Effective Dose (MED) and the Maximum Recoverable Volume (MRV).

The minimum effective dose is the minimum training necessary for the athlete to gain any positive adaptation. To help identify what the minimal effective dose, it is important to consider the habitual capacities of the athlete, which are the typical forces the athlete creates or is exposed to through their sport and training.

Maximum recoverable volume is the most training that the athlete can perform and recover from without a negative adaptive response (decreased performance).

Maximum Adaptive Volume sits somewhere between these upper and lower thresholds and is defined as the amount of training volume that yields the greatest performance improvements.

It is important to note that, while overload is necessary for driving adaptation, it must be balanced with adequate rest & recovery to promote adaptation and avoid injury. If the training load exceeds the athlete’s MRV, the likely outcomes are chronic performance decrease or injury.

Functional overreaching is the concept of overloading an athlete to the point of high fatigue and diminished performance. This is done with the intention of stimulating an enhanced supercompensation response that elevates performance beyond what would be possible at lower training loads. However, research indicates that performance improvements are greater when higher training loads are utilized prior to the taper while avoiding a state of functional overreaching (Aubry, Hausswirth, Louis, Coutts, & Le Meur, 2014).

When the athlete’s training load and overall stress far exceeds their capacity to recover, they may reach a state of overtraining. Intervention by both the athlete and the coach can help to avoid overtraining. On the coach’s side, it is important to monitor performance and adjust training loads when performance declines. 2.4 Special Considerations of Designing Training

Designing the Annual Plan

The following are guidelines for implementing the principles of training theory into the design of the annual plan of training:

Working Backwards

The first step in creating the annual plan is determining the dates of important competitions. These dates serve as the predetermined end point of the training plan by which the athlete needs to be operating at peak performance. Knowing the endpoint of the macrocycle is a necessity for determining when to progress from one phase to the next.

Logical Sequencing of Training

Training themes and emphases should be arranged in some logical sequence. Certain types of training have prerequisites that must be met, and sequencing should meet these prerequisites. Following are some general considerations for the logical sequencing of training themes over the course of the macrocycle.

1) General to Specific Progression

Over the course of the macrocycle, training should progress from general activities to specific activities. The general activities should serve to address some prerequisite for specific training. If a general training activity is not addressing some prerequisite for specific training, it should possibly be eliminated from the program.

2) Simple to Complex Progression

Training progresses from simple to complex. This is advantageous from both a coaching perspective and motor learning perspective. Ideally, the training is planned so that initial skills or movement patterns are the most easily learned and can serve as a framework for more complex skills or movement patterns to be utilized in later stages of training.

3) Volume to Intensity Progression

Training progresses from higher volumes of lower intensity work toward higher intensities for lower volumes. The higher initial training volumes are conducive to developing coordination and work capacity that support the execution of more intense training during later stages. Additionally, the decreasing volume in later stages of training helps to dissipate cumulative fatigue and reveal fitness improvements.

Strength Development
3.0 Introduction

In the Performance Phase the focus with strength training shifts to utilizing the acquired strength training skills to optimize strength levels according to the demands of the specific sport in question. The previous phases focus on developing a broad range of strength training skills and establishing a solid training history with progressive resistance training (e.g., from bodyweight only up to around 80-85% 1RM). In this phase, higher intensities are utilized to maximize strength development.

The age of the athletes entering this phase, is approximately 15 and 16 years old for females and males respectively. By this age both, in most cases, are post-Peak Height Velocity, and therefore are not experiencing such dramatic changes in height. However, they may still be increasing significant increases in body mass associated with Peak Weight Velocity (PWV). PWV is linked to increased lean muscle mass and fat mass for males and females, respectively.

Post-PHV the availability of anabolic hormones in the body now means it is possible to achieve both neural and structural adaptations to strength training. Physical maturity is accompanied by greater cognitive/psychological maturity, and the athletes generally display more motivation, better concentration, and attention to detail in the execution of more structured programmes and more technical/advanced exercises.

Due to the increased intensity of strength training in this phase, planned recovery periods with the macro-, meso- and micro-cycles become increasingly important. Similarly, proper nutrition and adequate sleep are vital. This is important to optimise recovery from the training that is of a greater intensity overall. Being physically mature, the athletes perform work at a much higher output and produce more energy anaerobically than prior to puberty, requiring more recovery between sets to recovery fully than pre-pubertal children whose energy production is primarily aerobic.

From a nutrition perspective, an increase in training frequency and volume increases energy expenditure and caloric and nutrient intake must meet the increasing demands of training and competition. Also, increased body size, especially with greater lean mass, adds to these energy demands.
