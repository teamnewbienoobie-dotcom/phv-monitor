The YPD model is adapted for males and females as gender differences in growth and maturation are also evident. This is because girls reach Peak Height Velocity (PHV) earlier than boys, at 11-12 years old versus 13-14 years old on average (Rogol, Roemmich, & Clark, 2002).

At puberty, when peak height velocity occurs, rapid changes in body size, shape and composition are experienced (Rogol et al., 2002). In fact, girls grow on average 9.0 cm/year at age 11-12 at PHV with a total height gain in puberty of ≈25cm (Marshall & Tanner, 1969). Boys instead, experience the growth spurt approximately 2 years later, with an average PHV of 10.3 cm/year and a total height gain of ≈28cm in the pubertal period (Marshall & Tanner, 1970).

AN INTRODUCTION TO LTAD
Long-term athletic development (LTAD) refers to the habitual development of “athleticism” over time to improve health and fitness, enhance physical performance, reduce the risk of injury and develop the confidence and competence of all youth and young athletes (R. S. Lloyd et al., 2016).

The LTAD process covers a broad spectrum of ages from childhood (up to approximately 11 in girls, and 13 in boys) and through adolescence (typically 12-18 years for girls and 14-18 years for boys) (R. S. Lloyd et al., 2016).

Developing athleticism has long been considered a pursuit for aspiring young athletes, but the recent NSCA position statement on LTAD (R. S. Lloyd et al., 2016), clearly states that coaches, teachers, strength and conditioning coaches, personal trainers and parents should be seeking to develop it for youth of all ages, abilities and aspirations. This appears to be more important than ever in the modern era.

Longitudinal research conducted over 30 years in Australia, observed 30,000 children aged 6-12 years old and showed a significant decline in a composite physical quotient (PQ) that was comprised of both a fitness and skill quotient. According to the results the PQ was 10-15 points over the three decades. The authors cited many contributing factors including increased sedentary behaviors with the emergence of modern technologies, lack of appropriately qualified teachers and coaches, as well as poorer provision (in terms of quality and quantity) of Physical Education programs (Tester, Ackland, & Houghton, 2014).

Athleticism is defined as the ability to repeatedly perform a range of movements with precision and confidence in a variety of environments, which require competent levels of motor skills, strength, power, speed, agility, balance, coordination, and endurance. The goal of LTAD programs should be to develop this broad array of athletic abilities, so that young people will be better physically and psychologically equipped to cope with and enjoy a lifetime of sport, physical activity and/or pursue a career in professional sport

## OLYMPIC LIFTING

**內容摘要**
OLYMPIC LIFTING

Olympic lifts and their derivatives give us an effective tool to optimize the development of strength, rate of force development and power output (Suchomel, et al., 2017). Across the Development phase, once the fundamental strength movements are well established, the athletes can also begin to learn to perform the Olympic weightlifting movements (or Olympic Lifts) with simple derivative exercises. Once the correct technique has been learnt the Clean & Jerk and Snatch provide excellent tools for power development at the strength-speed and speed-strength portions of the force-velocity curve, respectively.

At this stage of the athlete’s development the weightlifting movements also provide a new skill to learn and is welcomed diversion from what some may perceive as more repetitive strength work (squats, deadlifts etc) that they may have been doing for a couple of years. The athletes will also be more accustomed to working in a gym, as well as another year more mature and able to focus on more complex tasks. Introducing the weightlifting movements at this young age is often dependent on the nature/behaviour and size of the group. With Olympic bars being moved quickly overhead and dropped you need a group that is sensible enough and able to focus in the gym. If they’re not, the risks possibly outweigh the rewards. There are more simple, safer and equally effective options with the loaded jumps with which you can achieve the same outcome. 

Through the first year in the Development Phase you should be aiming to teach some simple derivatives, such as Jump Shrugs, Push Press, Drop Snatches progressing into Mid-thigh Cleans and Snatches from Hip. But the main emphasis will still be on establishing technical competency and developing relative strength in the major strength movements, where you will get the most bang for your buck (e.g. Squat, Split Squat, Bench Press, Pull Up, RDL). The second year of the Development phase is where you can aim to really build on the technical competencies in the Olympic Lifts (as long as the basic strength movements are technically sound). 

By establishing excellent Olympic lifting technique by the end of the Development Phase, it will be possible to progressively load these as the athlete moves into the Performance Phase. SUMMARY AND REFERENCES
SUMMARY
Agility training represents one of the most specific forms of training the athlete will undertake, if they play a running based sport.

Agility training ensures the transfer of training from the gym (i.e. strength and power) to the pitch.

Enhancing agility performance requires a rounded programme that delivers improvements in force production, technical ability in specific movement skills, linear speed and perception-cognitive factors.

Through the Development phase, it is necessary to refine and recalibrate the basic agility movements and help the athletes learn to combine these movements in typical patterns and combinations.

Rapid changes in size and speed at this age group, associated with PHV are the main drivers behind the need to refine and recalibrate agility skills.

The combination of increased speed and size lead to increased momentum that they will need to learn to control, as well as adapting to changes in leg length and the position of their centre of mass.

Be prepared to adapt and regress agility skills during this phase by reducing the entry velocity, simplifying tasks and revisiting the basic movements such as deceleration.

If time is limited, find other lessons or sessions where agility can be integrated through RAMP warm ups or in conjunction with technical-tactical components that ensure it is given the attention that it requires.

3.2 Planning strength training

Utilizing a periodized plan according to the athlete’s chosen sport is of greater importance than in the previous phases. The periodized plan needs to be developed working with the technical coach for the sport, developing a strength training programme that supports training and competition plan for the season. But also continues to develop the physical qualities they need at higher levels of their sport.

Within the first year of this phase, the periodization can still be relatively simple, and the aim should still be to build strength across the season, where possible. It can be a fine balance between pushing the physical capacities and ensuring they can still perform at the current level when it matters. Especially, in sports with short preparation periods and congested competition schedules.

However, we don’t want to overemphasise performance in competition at U17/U18, at the expense of developing the physical qualities required to succeed at an U20 and senior level. But on the other hand, we don’t want to overemphasise strength development to the point where the athlete is fatigued in competition, doesn’t perform when it matters, leading to deselection or missing out on the next contract or qualification event.

Therefore, it’s necessary to work closely as an interdisciplinary team to identify the windows of opportunity to maximise physical capacities such as strength, and when it’s necessary to allow them to maintain them and recover to perform.

This will involve:

Meeting with the coaching team to establish a clear annual plan
Identifying key competitions where the athlete needs to be able to perform at their best, where volume needs to be reduced.
Identifying less important competitions or periods that the athlete can train through and compete in a more fatigued state to maximize strength development.
Maximizing off-season and pre-season periods of time, especially in sports where there are congested in-season schedules.
Compatible and complimentary programme design that maximizes improvement, utilizing windows of time around training.

3.6 Summary

This chapter outlines the Strength Development process within the Performance Phase, emphasizing the transition from general strength training to sport-specific adaptations. It details how athletes, typically post-Peak Height Velocity (PHV) and more physically mature, can progress to training at higher intensity training loads (above 85% 1RM) to continue developing strength towards elite levels.

Key points:

Prerequisites for entry: Athletes should be post-PHV with a solid training history in strength training, demonstrating proficiency in key lifts (e.g., squat, deadlift, bench press) and meeting established benchmarks before advancing.

Strength Training Objectives: Developing maximal strength, hypertrophy, and muscular endurance tailored to the demands of the athlete’s sport. Conditioning work to mitigate injury risk is also important.

Periodization Strategies: Utilizing structured macro-, meso-, and micro-cycles, balancing strength development with competition performance.

Training Progression: Gradual intensification over time, with the first 1-2 years focusing on building maximal and relative strength through fundamental strength exercises.

Session Design & Exercise Selection: Sessions should be structured with speed and power work at the beginning of the session before strength training exercise. Heavy strength work should be completed with technically demanding exercises first. Accessory/muscle capacity exercises should be at the end of the session.

Velocity-based training: uses movement speed to prescribe and monitor strength training. VBT is used to monitor the intensity of prescribed work with target velocity ranges and identifying individual velocity profiles. It can also be used to monitor fatigue using velocity loss within a set and/or tracking velocity at set loads week to week.

Advanced Training Methods: Advanced strength training methods and training systems can be introduced to ensure continual adaptation and progression of strength level. This can include isometric, eccentric, and velocity-based training. It is important not to rely on advanced methods prematurely.

This chapter provides a comprehensive roadmap for strength development in young athletes, ensuring a balance between progressive overload, injury prevention, and peak performance alignment with competition schedules.
