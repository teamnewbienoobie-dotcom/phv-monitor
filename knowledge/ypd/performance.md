Training Theory, Periodization & Planning
Overview

Greater specialization and a desire to optimize or maximize physical performance in the Performance phase requires a much more comprehensive and structured approach to planning and delivering training programmes. This chapter outlines key concepts and principles related to training theory (the study of sport science to design athletic training), periodization and planning that serve as the basis for developing effective training plans.

2.0 Introduction to Periodization

Periodization is the process of planning training in order to produce high levels of performance at designated times. Periodization is characterized by dividing the training calendar into periods and phases which vary by emphases. These phases are sequenced such that each phase builds upon the adaptations and biomotor abilities developed in the previous phase.

Purpose in Athletic Development

In essence, periodization is planned variation in training. Planned variation serves two main purposes. It allows for continual progress by avoiding accommodation (diminished response to a repeated stimulus), as well as a reduction in the likelihood of overuse injury. Research has shown that planned variation has both direct and indirect effects leading to greater progress. When training volumes and intensities are matched, periodized training leads to larger fitness gains than non-periodized programs. However, planned variation also facilitates progression to higher training volumes and intensities while avoiding overtraining. This is important because increased training volumes and intensities have an even greater magnitude of effect on performance improvements (Rhea & Alderman, 2004).

Major Concepts

Planning is perhaps the most important step in ensuring the effectiveness of the training program. Planning enables a coach to control training variables and parameters in a manner that guarantees the best chance for success.

Record Keeping

Record keeping is another crucial part of the periodization process. Record keeping enables the coach to monitor training loads accurately, evaluate the effectiveness of the program, and make modifications to the training plan based on those observations.

3.1 Objectives

Specific objectives will be guided by the needs analysis of the sport, however some general objectives that are appropriate for strength training within this phase are below:

1) Continue to master major strength training movements, maintaining excellent technique under increasing loads (>85% 1RM)

2) Manipulate strength training prescription to bring about desired adaptation to meet the demands of the sport. This may include:

 a) Increasing relative or maximum strength in major strength training exercises, utilizing movements and muscle groups specific to the sport

b) Increasing muscle hypertrophy

c) Improving strength endurance

3) Developing the required levels of conditioning in specific muscle groups to withstand the demands of training and competing the sport, linked to common areas of injury identified in the needs analysis.

4) Introduce more advanced training systems and exercises, when appropriate and necessary to continue progression of strength development.

Expected outcomes:

Perform all major strength training exercises with excellent technique under high load.
Demonstrate high levels of relative/maximum strength in those exercises, in line with the benchmarks/expected levels in the sport.
Meet or exceed the minimum standards in specific muscle conditioning exercises or muscle capacity tests, that ensure a level of robustness for the sport.

3.4 Advanced Strength Training

Exercise selection

At the beginning of the Performance phase, the ‘Basic Strength Training’ exercises (listed below) with a gradual progression to higher intensities are more than sufficient to continue to develop higher levels of strength. The introduction of methods listed under ‘Advanced Strength Training’ should be carefully considered.

Whilst an athlete continues to demonstrate improvement in strength levels and key physical metrics (e.g. jump height, relative/peak power, sprinting speed) using basic exercises with progressive overload to higher intensities there is little need to introduce advanced variations. It’s important to remember that a novel training stimulus is never as potent as the first time it is used, therefore applying the right methods at the right time is key to continued progression and maximizing performance.

If an athlete has attained higher benchmarks for their key strength exercises (relative to their sport demands) and is no longer seeing progression in key physical performance metrics, then introducing more advanced methods of training can be considered to drive further progression. Training systems

Training systems are another variable that can be considered when trying to maximize different adaptations. The previous phases utilize basic training systems in the example programmes (e.g. multi-set, super-sets systems or circuits) this is to give athletes plenty of time to recover, and so not ask them to perform too many technically challenging or cognitively demanding tasks whilst they are still learning major strength exercises.
