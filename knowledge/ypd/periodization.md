## STRENGTH TRAINING SUMMARY

**內容摘要**
STRENGTH TRAINING SUMMARY
Strength is a key physical quality that positively impacts on other qualities such as power, speed and agility. It has been demonstrated that it is a safe and effective training method when delivered by appropriately qualified coaches and appropriate guidelines have been followed. A lot of effort should be put into planning, organizing the weight room to ensure the safety of the athletes, but also the efficiency and effectiveness of the session. Ensuring technical competency and strength versus bodyweight are in place first is important to the safe execution of traditional strength training exercise. We should always focus on developing technique before adding external load. The aim should be to develop balanced strength that covers a wide range of movements for the whole body. Coaches should avoid overemphasizing strength at the expense of other qualities, it should form part of a programme that also addresses speed, agility and power.
For further insights on long term strength development we highly recommend watching a master class from one of the world leaders in strength and power, Dr. Dan Baker. In this talk from our Child To Champion LTAD Conference in 2020, Dan will take you through his 6-stages of resistance training.

3.5 Monitoring Strength Development

Repetition Maximum Testing

Repetition maximum testing at the beginning and end of a training cycle is common practice. Tracking athletes’ strength development over time is important to ensure the prescription of loads (%1RM) is appropriate for the desired adaptation (e.g. maximum strength, hypertrophy, strength-endurance).

A 1 repetition maximum (1RM) is an assessment of maximal strength, but may not be the safest option for developing athletes. A safer alternative can be to estimate 1RM from a submaximal load (e.g. 3RM, 5RM or 10RM).

The Epley equation is a formula used to estimate an athlete’s 1RM based on the weight lifted and the number of repetitions performed. It is commonly used to assess maximal strength without specifically needing to perform a maximal 1RM test.

Use the following excel formula to calculate estimated 1RM:

Create a spreadsheet with 3 columns:

Column A: Weight lifted (kg)
Column B: Reps performed
Column C: Estimated 1RM

Within the column C, row 2 (C2) enter the formula: =A2*(1+(B2/30))

By utilizing the above method, it’s possible to continually re-calculate the estimated 1RM, this maybe most useful to do in the final week of a 4-week mesocycle when the intensity/load is greatest. The estimated 1RM can then be used to recalculate the loads for the subsequent mesocycle.

Isometric Testing
Where suitable equipment is available, such as force plates or more budget-friendly strain gauges, isometric strength testing would be the preferred method to assess changes in an athlete’s force producing capabilities given the greater insights that can be gained from these tests. As with RM testing, these tests can be utilized at appropriate time across training cycles and blocks to assess whether the desired changes have occurred from the prescribed training programme.

Common isometric test protocols include the isometric mid-thigh pull (IMTP) and isometric squat (IS) with these are generally considered to be better and safer methods to assessing maximal given the low skill requirement (vs. a true 1RM in a technical lift such as back squat), the high reliability of the measures and the variety of metrics that can be derived to understand the athletes current abilities (e.g. maximum force, rate of force development and left-right asymmetries [with dual force plates]).
