## PROGRAMMING POWER TRAINING

**內容摘要**
PROGRAMMING POWER TRAINING
Having outlined the different power training methods at our disposal it is important to explore how we can integrate these methods with each other as well as strength and speed training. 

PROGRAMMING POWER METHODS WITH STRENGTH TRAINING
The three programmes below provide examples of how all the different methods can be programmed into a session successfully. Across the programmes there is a progression in the complexity and intensity of the exercise as indicated by the colour coding. Strength training still forms the bulk of the S&C programme in the gym as that’s driving some major changes. But we are able to address reactive strength and power development with the integration of smaller doses of the other methods around the strength work.

POWER PROGRAMMING POWER METHODS WITH SPEED AND AGILITY TRAINING

Power methods are also highly compatible with speed training. Exercises can be selected with compatible directional biases that support learning to orientate force in the right direction and/or in similar times:

Acceleration programme - the power exercises have a forward/horizontal bias that help the athlete learn to project their centre of mass as they’ll need to when accelerating
Max velocity programme - the power exercises have a vertical bias and/or short contact times, as greater vertical force  is necessary in max velocity running. Fast SSC plyometrics are utilized to help the athlete learn to develop stiffness on the stance leg at top speed.
Agility programme - the power exercises are focused towards the frontal plane where the lateral agility movements occur. The lateral and diagonal leaps will allow the athletes to feel and learn how to project their body in the same direction they will need for the shuffle and cutting movements.

## SPEED TRAINING INVENTORY

**內容摘要**
SPEED TRAINING INVENTORY

As with the other key training areas, we have several different training methods at our disposal to develop the technical and neuromuscular components of sprinting. Different methods are more appropriate at different phases of the sprint, so we have separated them into Acceleration and Maximal Velocity training inventories.

As mentioned previously, sprinting is a skill and can be improved with high quality training. With appropriate sequencing of the different methods into a ‘speed curriculum’ we can consistently enhance our athlete’s sprinting ability. We will discuss the appropriate sequencing of methods further in this chapter.

MASTERCLASS VIDEOS - PRACTICAL AND PROGRAMMING FOCUS

For further ideas around speed training design, we highly recommend listening to the following sessions by Ruth Waghorn and Dr. Micheál Cahill from our Child To Champion Conference series.

Ruth Waghorn -  Teaching Speed For Team Sports

In this talk, Ruth takes us through her approach to developing speed with team sport athletes. Using 3 key concepts: ‘PROJECT’ ‘SWITCH’ ‘REACT’ to guide her programming and exercise selection from her time working with track and field athletes alongside Jonas Dodoo at Speedworks. Ruth and her colleague Liam Mistry took attendees through a range of exercises including technical drills, MB throws, plyometrics and resisted sprinting using the Exer-genie system. This is a great session to watch and expand your coaching toolbox!

Dr. Micheál Cahill - Resisted Sled Training: A Targeted Approach to Speed Development

In this talk, Micheál takes us through his research and practical guidance on Resisted sled training (RST). RST is a commonly used method of sprint specific training, however there is a lack of uniformity on training guidelines and best practice. RST can be used as a mode of horizontal strength training to complement a well rounded training program to improve speed. This presentation is aimed at providing valuable practical insight from evidence based research on young athletes to better equip coaches to assess, prescribe and coach RST to enhance speed. Of particular interest to practitioners dealing with young athletes will be the interaction of maturation and training competence on the phase of targeted sprint adaptation.

## OBSERVING AND ANALYZING MOVMENT

**內容摘要**
OBSERVING AND ANALYZING MOVMENT

Having a solid grasp of the key technical points outlined above and understanding what those movements should look like is critical to being able to observe, analyze and correct agility movements.

Often the best place to start is observing the athlete’s multi-directional movements in game play, not in closed drills. By observing it in live and reactive situations we get to see a lot more, and we can also begin to judge what needs addressing. There’s a tendency as coaches to want to ‘teach’ the movements from the off but a lot of these movements an athlete will do naturally and there may be very little need to explicitly teach the positions or we can do a better job of identifying what needs to be addressed within the different components of agility.These are some questions to ask yourself whilst observing agility movements:

1) Did the athlete executing the movement achieve the desired outcome? e.g. evading a defender, closing the space of the attacker.

If yes, maybe it’s not a movement we need to spend lots of time teaching and we can focus on other movements that do need work. Though it’s possible they succeeded at the task, despite having poor technique or decision making.

If they didn’t succeed, then we need to consider if the issue came because of technical, physical or perceptual-cognitive issues and poor decision making.

2) Was the technique of the movement in line with the technical model for the target movement pattern?

If not, maybe we need to take some time in closed drills to remove the noise and allow them to focus on getting in the correct positions.

If it was technically very good, then maybe we need to consider the decision making aspect. For example, if they executed a cut technically well, then maybe the problem is WHEN they decided to execute that movement. If they didn’t execute the cut with enough space between them and the defender they are more likely to get tackled.

3) Does the movement break down when they have to link it with other movement skills?

It is also possible that the movement breaks down because they were put in a situation where they had to link movements that they aren’t able to coordinate very well yet. If this is the case then we can focus on open drills that focus on combining the movements and make this more efficient.
